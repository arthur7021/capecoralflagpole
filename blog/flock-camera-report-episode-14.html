The Flock Camera Report

Published July 15, 2026

Cape Coral drivers have started waving at intersections like they know somebody up there — because, in a way, they do. A small solar-powered Flock Safety license plate reader camera has been quietly clocking every pass by the flagpole, and Burrowing Al decided to find out just how well it's gotten to know the neighborhood. He waves. He counts. He tries a mustache disguise. The camera, unmoved, keeps logging plate #7 on his clipboard anyway.

So what actually is a Flock camera?

Flock Safety's automated license plate readers (ALPRs) are the small solar-powered cameras popping up on poles across Cape Coral and the rest of Lee County — the same ones Burrowing Al is side-eyeing in this episode. According to mapping data from the community-run Deflock tracker, more than 200 of these cameras are now operating across Lee County, with dozens inside Cape Coral city limits. They photograph the backs of passing vehicles, log plates and vehicle characteristics, and feed that data to local law enforcement for investigations.

The Lee County Sheriff's Office has said it doesn't own or maintain the cameras itself but has investigative access to them, and it has declined to say publicly how many are installed or what they cost. That lack of detail is exactly what's fueled a growing privacy debate here — residents worried about a quiet, always-on record of who drives where, without a warrant in sight. It's the same debate playing out nationally: Flock Safety says its technology helps solve crimes, find missing people, and recover stolen vehicles, but the network has also drawn scrutiny over data-sharing practices and misuse elsewhere, and dozens of city contracts nationwide have already been cancelled or restricted over it.

Cape Coral hasn't said much publicly about its own Flock camera deployment either — which, frankly, tracks with how most of this flagpole story has gone. Burrowing Al's takeaway: if the cameras already know your license plate, your face, and your daily commute, the least you can do is wave back.
